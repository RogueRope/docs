Rope is a language. One of tension, stillness, surrender. It doesn't need applause. It doesn't need perfection. It needs presence.

- We don't separate the body from the spirit

2. **Tame It**  
   - Do the pre‚Äëwork: therapy session, hard conversation, breathwork, long run‚Äîwhatever helps you find steadiness.  
   - If it can't be fully solved, at least give it a safe container.

3. **Leave It**  
   - Some loads are too fresh, too sharp. That doesn't make you weak‚Äîjust honest. If the weight feels un‚Äëcarry‚Äëable, it might be kinder to sit this gathering out (or arrive later, in a different year).

- **Arriving with tension?** Name it together first. Decide: *Pause it, resolve it, or postpone the gathering.*

- **Don't process loudly in public zones.** Find a quiet corner or schedule time off‚Äësite.  
- **Consent to unload.** Ask before dumping heavy feelings on someone who may be tapped out.  
- **Use the support web wisely.** FWBs, Consent Crew, quiet spaces‚Äîthey're resources, not emotional dumpsters.

### Common Baggage Types

**Recent relationship changes:** Breakups, new relationships, shifting agreements, or major life transitions can all destabilize your emotional ground.

**Unresolved conflicts:** Ongoing tensions with existing partners, friends, or community members will likely surface in intimate spaces.

**Past trauma:** This gathering can trigger unexpected memories or reactions. Come prepared with your coping strategies.

**Shame or insecurity:** About your body, your experience level, your desires, or your worthiness of connection and pleasure.

**Scarcity fears:** Around attention, desirability, or access to the kinds of connections you want.

### Self-Care Strategies

- **Do your emotional homework** before arriving
- **Bring trusted coping mechanisms** (breathing techniques, grounding practices, etc.)

- **Plan for integration time** after the gathering to process what comes up

- You're in acute crisis or trauma recovery
- You have unresolved conflicts with others who will be attending
- Your relationship system is unstable and can't handle additional stress
- You're using the gathering to avoid or escape unaddressed life issues
- You don't have adequate emotional support systems outside the event

**After the gathering:**
- Process the experience with trusted friends or partners

- Integrate insights slowly rather than making major changes immediately

Remember: Boundary-setting is an act of care for both yourself and the community.

Kindly‚Äîand courageously‚Äîleave it at the door.

## FWBs: Friends With Benefits
*Yes, the good kind.*

At Bottoms Up!, care isn't something the organizers provide.
It's something **we all create** ‚Äî together, moment by moment.

This year, anyone can be an FWB. And we mean anyone.

### What Is an FWB?

**Friend With Benefits** = someone who's willing to show up for others.  
To hold space. To offer support. To check in. To notice.

Not all the time. Just when you feel able. Just when it's real.

FWBs aren't staff. They're **you**.  
And when you're ready to offer care, you show it by putting on a bracelet.

### How It Works

- **Bracelets = Consent to Care**  
  When you feel grounded and resourced enough to be available for others, slip on a FWB bracelet. That's your quiet signal: "I'm here if you need me."

- **No Pressure, No Performance**  
  You can take it off at any time. There's no expectation to stay "on" all weekend. Your own capacity matters just as much as anyone else's needs.

- **Anyone Can Step In**  
  This isn't a special role. It's a shared one. We all have moments where we can be the calm in the storm ‚Äî and moments when we can't.

### Why We're Doing It This Way

Because **care can't be outsourced**.  
Because the orga team can't hold it all ‚Äî and shouldn't.  
Because we believe in shared responsibility, not designated heroes.
**Because we want to see what happens when everyone at the event decides to care for each other.**

This bracelet system lets us flow in and out of service ‚Äî with consent, clarity, and freedom.

### What FWBs Offer

FWBs are available for:
- **Emotional check-ins** and active listening
- **Grounding support** during overwhelming moments
- **Basic aftercare** ‚Äî water, snacks, blankets, presence
- **Gentle guidance** for newcomers or anyone feeling lost
- **Conflict de-escalation** and helping people find appropriate resources
- **Just being a friendly face** when you need human connection

Remember: FWBs are peers, not professional counselors. We offer care within our capacity and knowledge, and we help connect people to more specialized support when needed.

Bottoms Up! can stir a lot.

### A Few Things That Might Help

- Be gentle with yourself. Take breaks. Drink water. Cry if you need to.  
- Listen to your body ‚Äî and let it guide your pace.  
- Set your boundaries clearly, and hold them with love.  
- Say no when you need to. Say yes only when it's real.  
- Focus on what feels *right* for you ‚Äî not what others are doing.

### Common Emotional Experiences

**Drop/Crash**: After intense scenes or connections, you might experience a sudden emotional or energetic low. This is normal. Rest, hydrate, seek gentle company or quiet solitude as needed.

**Overwhelm**: Too much stimulation, too many choices, too much intensity. Find a quiet space, practice breathing, limit sensory input.

**Loneliness in Connection**: Sometimes being around many people can make you feel more alone. This paradox is real and valid. Seek one-on-one connection or comfortable solitude.

**Processing Emotions**: Experiences here can bring up unexpected feelings ‚Äî old memories, new insights, complicated reactions. Let them move through you rather than trying to fix or analyze them immediately.

### When to Seek Support

Look for someone wearing a **FWB bracelet** when you need:

- Help connecting with resources or other support people

- A [Consent Crew member](/wellbeing/consent-safety) ‚Äî they're the dedicated team for boundaries, repair, or if something feels off

## Neurodivergent Support

### Neurodivergence Is Welcome. So Is Self-Knowing.

Bottoms Up! welcomes the full spectrum of how brains and bodies move through the world.

Autism, ADHD, anxiety, PTSD, sensory sensitivity, dyslexia, executive dysfunction, social burnout, panic loops, self-medication‚Ä¶  
Whatever you're navigating ‚Äî you're not too much, and you're not alone.

But we'll say this plainly:

**There is no dedicated neuro-support crew.**

There are no therapists, no designated liaisons.
Just a gathering full of people doing their best ‚Äî including you.

### What That Means

- **You are the expert on your needs.**  
  We trust you to know what helps ‚Äî and to bring it with you.

- **Support is peer-based and informal.**
  If you're struggling, reach out to someone you trust. That might be a friend, a partner, a fellow attendee.

- **Care flows in all directions.**  
  If you're able to support someone else, beautiful. If not, that's okay too. Don't overextend yourself to meet someone else's regulation needs. You don't have to.

- **The quiet space is open to all.**  
  No forms, no check-ins. Just a soft, low-stim place to decompress if the outside gets too loud.

### Resources We Provide

**Physical Accommodations:**
- Quiet, low-stimulation spaces for decompression
- Clear signage and information about space layouts
- Flexible participation ‚Äî you can engage at your own level
- Access to basic sensory tools (earplugs, eye masks, fidget items)

**Social Accommodations:**
- No pressure to participate in all activities
- Multiple ways to connect (verbal, non-verbal, structured, unstructured)
- Understanding that social energy is finite and needs to be managed
- FWB support system for gentle peer assistance

**Communication Support:**
- Clear, direct communication about expectations and guidelines
- Written information available for reference
- Permission to ask questions and seek clarification
- Understanding that processing styles vary

### Self-Care Strategies

Bring what you need:
- **Sensory regulation tools** (noise-cancelling headphones, weighted items, textures)
- **Executive function aids** (timers, schedules, reminder systems)
- **Emotional regulation supports** (comfort items, grounding techniques)
- **Communication tools** (scripts for difficult conversations, clear ways to express needs)

Remember: Taking care of your neurodivergent needs isn't selfish ‚Äî it's how you show up authentically for community and connection.

### A Note on Self-Medication

We understand that many neurodivergent people use various substances (prescription, over-the-counter, or otherwise) to manage symptoms and participate fully in life.

We ask only that you:
- Stay safe and within your known limits
- Remain able to give and receive consent
- Be honest about your capacity for various activities
- Ask for support if your regulation strategies aren't working

We don't police or judge how you manage your neurodivergence. We just want everyone to be able to participate safely and consensually.
