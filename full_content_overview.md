## Being Together: Our Shared Agreements

**We made a few agreements to keep this space honest, safe, and kind.**

- **Consent comes first, always.** Get it clearly, give it freely, and tune in to words and silence. Be grateful when someone trusts you with their truth.
- **Respect the humans around you.** Every person here carries a story. Be mindful, be curious, ask before touching, and honor pronouns.
- **Speak what's real.** Say what you mean and hear what's said. When things feel tender, choose clarity as a form of care.
- **Stay present.** Let the outside world wait. Be here with your body, your breath, and your attention.
- **Own your impact.** We all fuck up; what matters is what you do next. Apologize, repair, learn, and grow.
- **Play with awareness.** Kink, sex, and BDSM are welcome here, but never at the cost of safety or consent. Know your edges, respect others', and be gentle with power.
- **Take care of yourself.** Eat, hydrate, rest, and ask for what you need. You're not here to push through‚Äîyou're here to be whole.

### Generosity as Love in Action

**Be generous.**  
Offer what you can: your time, your skills, your mischief, your warmth.

What counts as participation? All of it.

- Chopping onions
- Hanging lights
- Tidying the space
- Holding someone who needs to cry
- Tying rope, sharing a song, offering your favorite snack at 3am

There is no competition here. No one gets a gold star. Contribution looks different for everyone ‚Äî and that's the point.

Whether you're a helper, a dreamer, a mischief-maker, or someone who simply holds the energy of care in a space... you belong.

## Who Belongs Here

This is a space for many.
But not for all.

Bottoms Up! welcomes queerness, neurodivergence, kink, softness, weirdness, and everything else that doesn't fit neatly into polite society. We hold space for shadow and play, for bodies that don't match expectations, for desires that don't ask permission.

We want you here if you come with curiosity.
We want you here if you long for honesty, connection, or the freedom to be as you are.
We want you here if you're ready to meet others with care, and to be met in return.

But let's be clear:

This is not a place for entitlement, for ego, or for domination without consent.
It's not a place for people who won't respect boundaries.
It's not a place for those unwilling to take responsibility for their impact.

If you're here to connect ‚Äî for real ‚Äî you're welcome.
If you're here to control, consume, or detach, you're not.

### Access Is Not the Same as Entitlement

We do our best to make this space financially and physically accessible.
**If money is the barrier, reach out.** We're ready to talk and find something that works.

Maybe that's a discount.
Maybe installments.
Maybe a trade.

We don't do shame. We do dialogue.

But access is a _shared responsibility_.
You still need to show up with presence. To tend to yourself and the people around you. To be ready for the intensity this space brings.

If you're not sure you're ready ‚Äî that's okay.
Better to pause than to force it.

### The Intensity Is Real

This isn't a light weekend. It's immersive, emotional, and sensory. We support pacing and self-care: you're encouraged to be honest about what you can hold in community right now.

## The Flow of Our Days

**A rough outline. We'll adjust to what the camp needs.**

This isn't a scheduled program. It's a gentle container for what wants to emerge when people show up fully present, ready to connect, and willing to be real.

---

## Wednesday Afternoon ‚Äì Build Begins

Come early if you can.
Not because we need help (though we do), but because this is one of the best ways to arrive.

There's something sacred about shaping the space before it holds us.
Hanging lights. Painting signs. Arranging the lounge areas.
Making coffee. Making eye contact. Making meaning.

No skills required. Just hands. Just heart. Just presence.

---

## Wednesday ‚Äì We Land

Arrival day is shared. **Everyone is expected to arrive by Wednesday evening.** Settle in, let your body catch up, let the outside world fall away.

As night falls we move into Opening Circle ‚Äî a threshold ritual rather than a lecture. We breathe together, ground in consent, and agree on the texture of the days ahead. From there, play begins softly and organically.

We ask you to begin with us. But you don't have to stay until the end.

Need the hour-by-hour? ‚Üí [Schedule](/docs/practical/schedule)

If you find yourself overwhelmed, exhausted, complete ‚Äî **it's okay to leave early**.  
We honor your limits. We celebrate your self-awareness.  
Tapping out is not failure. It's wisdom.

You know yourself best. We trust you.

---

## Thursday to Saturday ‚Äì We Unfold

We begin Thursday morning with our **Consent Moment** ‚Äî  
a guided, embodied check-in.  
Not just reminders, but rituals.  
It's mandatory. And it's meaningful.

From there, the days stretch open.

Jam spaces. Lounge whispers. Quiet rituals. Late-night laughter.
Rope in the space. Kisses in the dim light. Gentle movements. Soft eyes. Stillness. Play.

There will be peer-led offerings ‚Äî nothing scheduled yet, everything possible.  
And we won't leave it all to chance.

---

## Sunday ‚Äì We Unravel

For many, this is the soft close.
We gather our things. Tears come up.
We hold each other. We say goodbye.

But not everyone makes it to Sunday ‚Äî and that's okay.

If you leave early, we'll miss you.
And we'll honor your choice.

Some people stay the whole time.
Some people leave halfway through.
Both are valid. Both are brave.

What matters is that you came at all.

---

Yes, we love rope.  
But that's just the surface.

This is a space to play ‚Äî with power, with surrender, with sensation, with self.

## What Sex-Positive Means (For Us)

**Not:** Porn-centric performance, exploitation, male-gaze spectacle, pressure to be sexual, crude or fetishy stereotypes.

- Desire is normal, worthy, and beautiful

- Consent makes it ethical
- Mystery and sensuality matter more than explicitness
- We don't separate the body from the spirit

## Why It Matters

Most rope communities are built by and for people who had to hide their sexuality to stay safe. Rope was refuge‚Äîa place where desire could exist, but quietly. Gratefully. Apologetically.

We've inherited that caution.

But something shifts when we stop apologizing for desire. When we say openly: **Yes, sexuality is part of what we practice here. Yes, people will kiss, touch, make love, express pleasure. Yes, nudity happens. Yes, we light candles and we also light fire.**

That shift creates permission. Room. Freedom.

If you've been told your desire is too much, too weird, too kinky, too queer, too anything‚Äîthis space says: **Not here. Here, your hunger is welcome.**

**At the gathering, you might see:**

- Naked bodies being bound, touched, witnessed

- Intimacy that's tender, fierce, slow, urgent, playful, solemn

**You won't see:**

- Pressure to perform
- Objectification
- Spectacle for its own sake
- Exploitation

**You will feel:**

- Presence
- Consent
- Reverence for bodies and desire
- The sacred in the sensual

---

## The Distinction We Make

We're **not** a sex party. We're not a porn set. We're not a place where sexuality is the entertainment for voyeurs.

We're a space where people can explore sexuality **with dignity, presence, and care**. Where desire is woven into rope, into ritual, into connection.

Sex can be:

- A spiritual practice
- An expression of power and vulnerability
- A form of intimacy
- A way to be fully alive in your body
- Joyful, tender, intense, playful, sacred

All of this is welcome. And so is NOT participating. Your sexuality is yours‚Äîto express, to explore, or to keep private.

When we bind someone, we're asking: _Can you trust me with your body? With your desire? With your truth?_

That trust is erotic. It's also sacred. Both things at once.

Trying to do rope without acknowledging sexuality is like trying to do rope while pretending bodies don't exist. You're hiding the most important part.

We don't hide. We celebrate.

- Violate someone's consent
- Pressure anyone into sexuality
- Detach from presence (sex as performance is not welcome)
- Treat bodies as objects

Most rope communities exist in the margins, grateful for the space, not pushing back against the culture that says desire should be hidden.

We're building something different: a community where sexuality is named, honored, celebrated. Where you don't have to choose between being kinky and being respectable. Where desire and reverence live in the same moment.

This is rare.

That's why you're here.

## Before You Arrive

Take a breath. You're about to cross a threshold.

Maybe you're nervous. Maybe you're excited. Maybe both at once.
That's exactly right. This is a big moment ‚Äî you're choosing vulnerability, connection, presence.

**You belong here.**

This page will walk you through what happens when you arrive, where to go, who you'll meet, and how the first 24 hours unfold. We've got you.

---

## Wednesday Afternoon: Build Day (Optional, Recommended)

**Arrival window:** Anytime from 14:00 onwards

If you can come early, **please do.** Build Day is one of the most beautiful ways to arrive.

There's something intimate about shaping the space together before it holds us. Hanging fairy lights. Arranging cushions. Setting up play areas. Making coffee. Making eye contact. Making meaning.

**You don't need skills. You don't need to know what you're doing.**
Someone will hand you a task. You'll work alongside strangers who won't be strangers for long.

By the time Opening Circle begins, you'll already have your first inside jokes, your first shared glances, your first sense of *oh, these are my people.*

### What to Do When You Arrive for Build Day

1. **Park and find the main entrance** ‚Äî Look for welcome signs
2. **Check in at the registration table** ‚Äî We'll have a list with your name
3. **Get your welcome pack** ‚Äî Includes your room assignment, schedule, and name tag
4. **Drop your bags in your room** ‚Äî Find your bunk, claim your space
5. **Come back to the common area** ‚Äî Someone will guide you to whatever needs doing

No pressure. No performance. Just presence.

---

## Wednesday Evening: Main Arrival

**Arrival deadline:** 21:00 (for Opening Circle)

Most people arrive Wednesday evening between 18:00‚Äì20:30. You'll see cars pulling in, people hauling bags, nervous laughter, tentative hellos.

### The Check-In Process

**Step 1: Find Registration (18:00‚Äì20:30)**

Look for the registration table near the main entrance. You'll see:
- A table with a sign that says "Welcome" or "Check-In"
- One or two friendly faces with clipboards
- Other arrivals milling about, looking as nervous as you feel

**Step 2: Check In**

Tell us your name. We'll:
- Check you off the list
- Give you a **welcome pack** (room assignment, schedule, venue map, name tag)
- Answer any immediate questions ("Where's the bathroom?" "Which room am I in?")
- Point you toward your accommodations

**Step 3: Settle Into Your Room**

Find your room number (on your welcome pack). You'll be in a shared bunk room with 4‚Äì8 other people.

- Claim a bunk (first-come, first-served)
- Unpack what you need for the night
- Meet your roommates (or not ‚Äî some people need quiet time first)
- Change into comfortable clothes if you want

**Step 4: Explore (If You Have Time)**

Before Opening Circle begins at 21:00, wander a little:
- Find the bathrooms
- Locate the kitchen/common area
- Peek into the play spaces (they'll be empty, waiting)
- Breathe. Let your nervous system catch up.

You don't have to talk to anyone yet if you're not ready.
Just let the space start to feel familiar.

## Who's Who: Finding Support & Connection

One of the most common questions: **"Who do I talk to if I need help?"**

Here's how to identify key people:

### FWBs (Friends With Bracelets)

Anyone wearing a **colored bracelet** is a **Friend With Bracelet (FWB).**

This means they're open to:
- Holding space for a check-in
- Listening without fixing
- Helping you find resources
- Offering a grounding presence

**You can become an FWB too.** Bracelets will be available throughout the event. When you feel resourced enough to care for others, put one on.

**How to approach an FWB:**
- "Hey, I see you're wearing a bracelet. Do you have a minute?"
- "I'm feeling a bit overwhelmed ‚Äî could we sit for a moment?"
- "I'm new and not sure how to [ask someone to play / find the quiet room / etc.] ‚Äî can you help?"

### Consent Crew

Consent Crew members will be introduced at **Opening Circle.** They're here for:
- Boundary violations or consent concerns
- Conflict mediation
- Safety issues
- Situations that feel off or unsafe

**How to identify them:** They'll wear specific identifying markers (we'll explain at Opening Circle).

**How to reach them:**
- Find them in person (ask anyone if you're not sure who they are)
- Email: consent-crew@roguerope.be
- Emergency contact (shared at Opening Circle)

### Organizers

The folks who built this space. We'll introduce ourselves at Opening Circle.

**We're here for:**
- Logistics questions (food, accommodations, schedules)
- Venue issues (heating, access, supplies)
- General "I don't know who to ask" questions

You'll see us moving through the space, checking in, making coffee, adjusting things.

### Everyone Else

Most people here are just like you ‚Äî nervous, excited, figuring it out.

Some have been before. Some haven't.
Some are confident. Some are quiet.
All of them are here because they chose connection over comfort.

**You can talk to anyone.** More on that below.

## Opening Circle (Wednesday 21:00) ‚Äî Mandatory

This is the threshold ritual. **Everyone must be present.**

### What Is It?

Opening Circle is our collective landing. It's where we:
- Breathe together for the first time
- Ground in consent and shared agreements
- Meet each other's eyes
- Set the container for the days ahead

**It's not a lecture. It's a ritual.**

### What Happens

- We'll gather in a circle (literally)
- Facilitators will guide us through:
  - A grounding practice (breathing, feeling our bodies, arriving)
  - Introduction to key people (organizers, Consent Crew)
  - Consent agreements (enthusiastic, ongoing, collective)
  - Practical info (where things are, daily rhythm, how to ask for help)
  - Community agreements (how we care for each other)
- We'll share brief introductions (just your name, no need for a speech)
- We'll set an intention together

**How long:** ~60‚Äì90 minutes

**What to bring:** Just yourself. Wear something comfortable. Sit on the floor or grab a cushion.

### After Opening Circle

When the circle closes, the gathering truly begins.

Some people will drift to the play spaces. Some will stay in the lounge and talk. Some will make tea and sit quietly. Some will go to bed early, overwhelmed and needing rest.

**All of it is okay.**

There's no "right" way to start. Follow what feels true.

## Your First Conversations

**"How do I even start talking to people?"**

We hear you. This is one of the hardest parts.

### You Don't Have to Be Interesting

You don't need a clever opening line. You don't need to perform.

Here are some honest, easy ways to start:

**Simple openers:**
- "Is this your first time here?"
- "How are you feeling so far?"
- "I'm [name]. I have no idea what I'm doing." (People love honesty.)
- "Do you know where the [coffee / quiet room / bathroom] is?" (Even if you do.)
- "Can I sit here?" (Then just sit. Sometimes presence is enough.)

**If you're nervous, say so:**
- "I'm feeling a bit shy ‚Äî is it okay if I just sit with you quietly?"
- "I'm working up the courage to talk to people. You're my first attempt."

Vulnerability is currency here. Use it.

### If You're Coming Solo

You're not alone ‚Äî about 30% of attendees come solo.

**Ways to connect:**
- Come to Build Day (easiest way to make early connections)
- Sit in the common area with a cup of tea (people will find you)
- Ask someone at dinner if you can join them
- Offer to help with something (dishes, tidying, setting up)
- Attend the Consent Moment on Thursday morning (everyone will be there)

**You will find your people.** It might not happen in the first hour. That's okay.

By day 2, you'll have inside jokes. By day 4, you'll have people who feel like family.

### If You're Coming With a Partner

You might be tempted to stay close to them. That's natural.

But also: **let yourself wander.**

Part of the magic here is meeting people outside your usual orbit. Your partner will still be there. And you'll have more to share when you reconvene.

Talk beforehand about:
- How much time you want together vs. apart
- Whether you'll check in at meals or just find each other organically
- What kind of connection (flirting, play, etc.) feels okay with others

Jealousy might come up. That's normal. More on that in [Partners](/docs/wellbeing/partners).

## The First Night

### After Opening Circle: What Now?

**Option 1: Stay up and explore**
- Hang out in the lounge
- Wander into a play space and watch (or ask to play)
- Make new friends over tea
- Flirt. Connect. Be soft.

**Option 2: Go to bed early**
- You've traveled. You're tired. Your nervous system is processing.
- Sleep is sacred. No one will judge you for resting.

**Option 3: Find a quiet corner**
- Process alone for a bit
- Journal, breathe, cry if you need to
- Come back to the group when you're ready

All of these are valid. Honor what you need.

### Sleep Logistics

**Shared rooms:**
- Bunk beds (bring earplugs if you're a light sleeper)
- People will come and go at different hours
- Some folks stay up late; some wake early
- Respect quiet hours as best you can

**If you can't sleep:**
- Wander to the common area (there's always someone awake)
- Make tea
- Sit by the fire
- Read, write, be

**If you're overwhelmed:**
- Find the quiet room
- Find an FWB (someone with a bracelet)
- Step outside for air
- It's okay to feel a lot. This space can hold it.

We share these rooms like we share scenes: with care, attention, and a sense that someone else will arrive after us. Notice what needs doing and do it if you can.

**Sleeping areas** feel restful when bunks stay loosely organized, clothes live in bags instead of on floors, and shared items boomerang back to where everyone expects them.

**Bathrooms** move faster when showers stay short, surfaces get a quick swoosh, and anything disposable lands in the bins provided (menstrual products included).

## Why This Exists

People come to Bottoms Up! to be real ‚Äî soft, ecstatic, messy, reverent. That kind of openness only happens when we know the room is safe. Cameras are welcome **only** when they honor that trust.

---

## Core Rule: Explicit Consent, Every Time

- Ask **before** you take a photo or video  
- Ask **everyone** in the frame ‚Äî foreground, background, even blurry shapes  
- State how you intend to use it (personal memento? private album? might share later?)  
- Accept "no" immediately and without persuasion  
- Be ready to delete in the moment if someone changes their mind

_Blur is not consent. Assumption is not consent._

---

## How to Ask Without Disrupting

- Approach people when they‚Äôre not in the middle of a scene  
- Keep it simple: ‚ÄúCan I capture this for my personal memory?‚Äù  
- If it‚Äôs a group shot, get a verbal yes from **every** person  
- If anyone hesitates, treat it as a no  
- Put the camera away during sensitive rituals and consent moments

---

## What‚Äôs Not Allowed

- Bystander shots, candids of people you didn‚Äôt ask  
- Flash, loud shutters, or anything that breaks the scene  
- Filming scenes you‚Äôre not directly part of  
- Recording audio without explicit consent  
- Using cameras in bathrooms, changing areas, or private rooms

If you‚Äôre unsure whether something is okay, skip it or check with an organizer.

---

## Respect the Space

The venue is part of our privacy. When framing photos:

- Avoid wide shots that reveal location details, signage, or maps  
- Crop or blur identifying features if in doubt  
- Never capture staff or community members who haven‚Äôt opted in

---

## After the Gathering

- Re-confirm with every person in the image before you share it anywhere  
- Keep the venue anonymous: no names, addresses, or identifiable architecture  
- Remove posts immediately if anyone asks you to delete them  
- Skip tagging people unless they explicitly request it  
- Treat community albums as confidential ‚Äî no screenshots or forwarding without consent

---

## If Something Feels Off

Tell the Consent Crew or organizers right away. We‚Äôd rather have an awkward conversation than a broken container. Your instinct to protect the space is part of the culture here.

## Understanding Consent

### The Short Version?

If it feels like you're selling something ‚Äî stop.
If you're working too hard for a "yes,"
pause.
Step back.
Try again ‚Äî or maybe, don't.

A real yes doesn't need convincing.

**Trust us ‚Äî that kind of yes leads to better everything.**

### Celebrate the No

Someone says no?
Good. That means they trust you enough to be honest.

Say thank you.
Mean it.

A "no" is not a rejection ‚Äî it's a reveal.
It shows you something real: that this person feels safe enough to voice their boundary.
That's not a failure of connection ‚Äî that's the foundation of it.

When someone says no, it proves they're present.
It proves they're paying attention to what they want ‚Äî and what they don't.
And when we receive that no with grace, we show we're worthy of trust.

Every healthy dynamic ‚Äî sexual, sensual, kinky, or otherwise ‚Äî is built on the ability to **hear no, hold it, and stay open**.

So don't rush past it. Don't shrink from it.
Celebrate it.

Because if someone can say no, it means that when they say yes‚Ä¶
they really mean it.

And that's the kind of yes worth waiting for.

> "Consent isn't a question. It's a state.

- A yes to one thing is not a yes to everything.

A *shared*, excited, embodied yes.
The kind that moves you both.

## Consent in Practice

### Ongoing Consent

Consent is not a one-time agreement. It's a continuous conversation that includes:

- **Check-ins during play** ‚Äî "How are you feeling?" "What do you need?"
- **Reading non-verbal cues** ‚Äî body language, breathing, muscle tension
- **Honoring changes** ‚Äî energy shifts, emotional responses, physical limits
- **Aftercare discussions** ‚Äî processing what happened, how it felt

### When Things Get Complicated

Sometimes consent gets murky. Sometimes we misread signals or cross lines we didn't know existed. Sometimes we realize mid-scene that we're not as okay as we thought we were.

That's human. That's normal. What matters is how we respond:

- **Stop immediately** when someone says stop
- **Check in regularly** during intense scenes
- **Take responsibility** when you've overstepped
- **Seek support** when you need help processing

- **Use our support systems** (Consent Crew, FWBs) when needed
- **Take breaks** when you need them
- **Respect the container** we've created together

Remember: Safety isn't about avoiding all risk ‚Äî it's about taking conscious, consensual risks with people who care about your wellbeing as much as their own.

## Before You Cross the Threshold

Every one of us carries stories: heartbreaks, unresolved fights, jealousy, shame, grief, burnout.
That's human. It's also **volatile tinder** in a space like Bottoms Up!‚Äîwhere intensity, intimacy, and erotic charge run high.

Here's the truth:

> **Whatever you bring in‚Äîlight or dark‚Äîwill get amplified.**
> If it's wonder, the place will glow.
> If it's unspoken resentment, it can overflow into disruption.

This is a loving caution. A reminder to **pause, scan, and sort** your inner luggage before you enter the space.

### Name It, Tame It, or Leave It

1. **Name It**
   - Say it out loud (to yourself, a friend, a journal): _"I'm still angry at X."_
   - Hidden baggage loves the dark; naming switches on the light.

2. **Tame It**
   - Do the pre‚Äëwork: therapy session, hard conversation, breathwork, long run‚Äîwhatever helps you find steadiness.
   - If it can't be fully solved, at least give it a safe container.

3. **Leave It**
   - Some loads are too fresh, too sharp. That doesn't make you weak‚Äîjust honest. If the weight feels un‚Äëcarry‚Äëable, it might be kinder to sit this gathering out (or arrive later, in a different year).

### Relationship Storms & Drama Triangles

- **Arriving with tension?** Name it together first. Decide: _Pause it, resolve it, or postpone the gathering._
- **Love triangles & undefined connections?** Get explicit now. Vagueness becomes violence when the energy spikes.
- **Recent break‚Äëups?** If seeing this person tie someone else might rip you apart, love yourself enough to wait.

This gathering isn't therapy. It's not neutral ground. It's a **magnifier**.

### Respect the Shared Space

This space holds many bodies, many hearts.
Personal storms can swirl into collective weather.

- **Don't process loudly in public zones.** Find a quiet corner or schedule time off‚Äësite.
- **Consent to unload.** Ask before dumping heavy feelings on someone who may be tapped out.
- **Use the support web wisely.** FWBs, Consent Crew, quiet spaces‚Äîthey're resources, not emotional dumpsters.

### Common Baggage Types

**Recent relationship changes:** Breakups, new relationships, shifting agreements, or major life transitions can all destabilize your emotional ground.

**Unresolved conflicts:** Ongoing tensions with existing partners, friends, or community members will likely surface in intimate spaces.

**Past trauma:** This gathering can trigger unexpected memories or reactions. Come prepared with your coping strategies.

**Shame or insecurity:** About your body, your experience level, your desires, or your worthiness of connection and pleasure.

**Scarcity fears:** Around attention, desirability, or access to the kinds of connections you want.

### When to Consider Not Attending

Sometimes the most caring choice is to wait. Consider postponing if:

- You're in acute crisis or trauma recovery
- You have unresolved conflicts with others who will be attending
- Your relationship system is unstable and can't handle additional stress
- You're using the gathering to avoid or escape unaddressed life issues
- You don't have adequate emotional support systems outside the event

This isn't rejection ‚Äî it's wisdom. The gathering will exist again, and you can arrive when you're more resourced.

**Processing Waves**

- Say no when you need to. Say yes only when it's real.

- **Talk to your person privately.** Don't process in public or through other people
- **Use the support systems.** FWBs can help you sort through unexpected feelings
- **Remember your agreements.** Return to what you decided together before arriving
- **Be gentle with each other.** This intensity can bring up old wounds or new insights

### When You Need Backup

You don't have to hold wobbles alone.

- Look for someone wearing an [FWB](support-care) bracelet if you need grounding or a compassionate witness
- Reach out to the [Consent Crew](consent-framework) for boundary repair, mediation, or if something feels off
- Use the quiet spaces, go for a walk, or find a soft corner where you can land together
- Ask trusted friends or constellation members for support if you already have shared agreements

Let the community catch you when you need it. That's why we're all here.

### Relationship Configurations

**Monogamous couples:** You're welcome here whether you play only with each other or not at all. The gathering offers many forms of connection beyond physical play.

**Non-monogamous folks:** Great! Communicate clearly with existing partners and new connections about boundaries and expectations.

**Solo but partnered:** Coming alone while partnered elsewhere requires extra care in communication both directions.

**Single and exploring:** You're not expected to find a partner here, but if connections emerge, navigate them with the same care as everyone else.

**Relationship anarchists:** We love your approach to connection, just be clear about what you're offering and seeking.

And stay open to what's possible.

### Helpful Practices

- **Name your needs.** Out loud. Early. Kindly.
- **Check in daily.** Morning coffee or end-of-day recaps work wonders.

## How Care Flows Here

Care at Bottoms Up! doesn't live with one team. It's a web: FWBs and friends, the Consent Crew, quiet spaces, conscious care of the venue, and organizers backing it all up.

Care isn't something the organizers provide.
It's something **we all create** ‚Äî together, moment by moment.

This page shows you how the support systems work and how to plug into them.

## Friends With Benefits (FWBs)
_Yes, the good kind._

### What Is an FWB?

**Friend With Benefits** = someone who's willing to show up for others.
To hold space. To offer support. To check in. To notice.

Not all the time. Just when you feel able. Just when it's real.

FWBs aren't staff. They're **you**.
And when you're ready to offer care, you show it by putting on a bracelet.

This year, anyone can be an FWB. And we mean anyone.

### How It Works

- **Bracelets = Consent to Care**
  When you feel grounded and resourced enough to be available for others, slip on a FWB bracelet. That's your quiet signal: "I'm here if you need me."

- **No Pressure, No Performance**
  You can take it off at any time. There's no expectation to stay "on" all weekend. Your own capacity matters just as much as anyone else's needs.

- **Anyone Can Step In**
  This isn't a special role. It's a shared one. We all have moments where we can be the calm in the storm ‚Äî and moments when we can't.

### Why We're Doing It This Way

Because **care can't be outsourced**.
Because the orga team can't hold it all ‚Äî and shouldn't.
Because we believe in shared responsibility, not designated heroes.
**Because we want to see what happens when everyone at the gathering decides to care for each other.**

This bracelet system lets us flow in and out of service ‚Äî with consent, clarity, and freedom.

### What FWBs Offer

When someone wears the bracelet, they're saying:

- **I can hold emotional check-ins** and really listen
- **I can help you ground** if things feel overwhelming
- **I can offer basic aftercare** ‚Äî water, snacks, blankets, quiet presence
- **I can walk you through the space** if you're new or feeling lost
- **I can help de-escalate conflict** and connect you to other resources
- **I can be a friendly face** so you don't have to navigate a moment alone

FWBs are peers, not therapists or security. We help within our capacity and loop in the [Consent Crew](consent-framework) or orga when more support is needed.

### When You Might Reach for an FWB

- You're feeling wobbly and need grounding
- You need help navigating a tricky emotional moment
- You're not sure who to talk to about something that feels off
- You just need someone to walk with for a minute

FWBs aren't therapists or security. They're fellow participants.
If more support is needed, they'll help connect you with the [Consent Crew](consent-framework) or core team.

## Conscious Care of Space

### Care is not just what we do for each other ‚Äî
it's what we do *with* each other.

Bottoms Up! isn't staffed. It's shared. That means the space only holds us as well as **we** hold it.

This isn't about being tidy.
It's about being *conscious*.
Of your body. Your stuff. Your impact. The people beside you.

### The Art of Noticing

You don't need a chore list. Just awareness.

- **Notice what's yours.**
  If you brought it ‚Äî take it home. If you used it ‚Äî clean it. If you dropped it ‚Äî pick it up.

- **Notice what's off.**
  If something looks forgotten, lost, or misplaced, take initiative. Straighten it. Stash it. Set it aside safely. You don't need permission to help.

- **Notice the little things.**
  Wipe a surface. Carry a cup. Return a chair to its place. Small actions ripple.

- **Notice each other.**
  If someone leaves a trace behind ‚Äî a cup, a shirt, a tool ‚Äî offer a gentle reminder. Not judgment. Just care.

### We Are the Care Crew

You don't have to be a cleaner to care.
You don't have to be a "helper" to help.

Care isn't a task ‚Äî it's a way of being in relationship with space, self, and others.

Let's leave every room better than we found it.
Let's make our messes part of the magic.
Let's take responsibility for what we touch, use, open, move, spill, or stir.

That's the kind of kink we're into.

**Care is contagious. Let's start the ripple.**
